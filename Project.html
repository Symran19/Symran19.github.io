Socioeconomics and Voting Behaviours:

Analysing the modern-day relationship between socioeconomic factors and UK voting behaviours

Socioeconomic factors have typically been seen as the most dominant influences on voting behaviours, over the last few decades, given that it would be rational for individual's political preferences to align with their positions in the social structure. This project aims to analyse whether socioeconomic factors are still relevant and able to change/understand voting patterns today, as I focus on the most recent 2019 election; with comparative reference to 2017.

The Conservatives won a 365-seat majority (+66 on 2017), crumbling Labour's 'red wall' across the North and Midlands, as former 'Labour loyalists' swung to the opposite side of the political spectrum (Source1 and Source2). This unprecedented behaviour leads me to hypothesis that socioeconomic factors, won't have the same kind of dominance seen at previous elections.

Due to the maps being quite detailed, I tried to make them more user-friendly by adding a zoom function - but unfortunately, vega didn't permit this to work.

Opinion Polls

This chart illustrates the most important issues for the public, when voting in 2019. I decided to compare their importance between the 2017 and 2019 elections.

Evidently, the public lacks concern for social issues. In fact, 'Economy/Jobs/Unemployment/Growth' and 'NHS/Hospitals', seem to be the only responses that directly correlate to socioeconomic factors, where others, like Brexit and Immigration, are likely highly placed due to being prominent timely issue.

Both years' datasets were in accessible CSV formats (PollData-2017 and PollData-2019) and cleaned using Python. But, when it came to merging them, the response phrasings differed slightly. Therefore, I decided to manually input the corresponding 2017 data, since there were minimal data points.

Education

Many assume: the more educated a constituency, the more likely they are to vote Labour. To test this, I regressed the percentage of degree holders in Labour and Conservative winning constituencies (Data_Source) against their respective 2019 vote shares (Data_Source), which resulted in no detectable relationship (Excel-Merged-Dataset).

Wealth and Earnings

This section looks at wealth/earning inequality from a regional context.

With average wealth being the highest in southern regions and weekly earnings being the lowest in the north, these charts heavily support the North-South divide. Since the Conservative campaign placed great emphasis on their 'levelling-up' agenda, this likely swayed many northerners to support their cause, and plausibly supports an earnings-voting relationship.

Finding a relevant API proved quite difficult since this project focuses on past events. But I eventually decided to embed ONS APIs regarding earnings by industry, using loops to batch download them (Python). I then concatenated each series in vega and associated each region to their top industry employer (Data_Source). This visualisation was a second-resort approach, after being unable to layer all the series onto one chart, as vega had trouble reading the data and the datasets had no identifiable attributes to create a key.

For the second chart, the ONS supplied this data in a tidy CSV format (Data_Source), so only basic Excel date cleaning was done. However, further research was required regarding the line-segment, which represents the 2019 election, as vega refused to place it correctly.

Class and Age

Since Brexit, age has presented to be quite a vote divider, perhaps more dividing than the once renowned influence, class.

Previous elections have implied that lower social classes side Labour and older individuals, Conservative. This assumption appears correct age-wise but not entirely true for class. As the age ranges increase for both classes, as does their share of Conservative supporters. Despite 63% of young DE voters favouring Labour, overall Labour's hold on this class is starting to decline, as Conservatives hold 41% of the DE vote share to Labours 39%.

The data was scraped from Ipsos, and cleaned in Python, with minor multi-index issues which I fixed by joining the indexes together (.join) and then dropping them.

Ethnicity

I concluded the most efficient way to present ethnicity was to illustrate the ethnic breakdowns of selected UK districts, where over half of the constituencies within each district swung political alliance.

Previous elections exhibited that more diverse areas typically voted Labour. Given all the districts above swung majoritarily Conservative, it's evident that ethnicity isn't following the designated assumptions; so, implying a correlation would be difficult.

This data was scraped (Data_Source) and converted into tidy format using Python.

Conclusions

Although some of the factors investigated, do propose a relationship with voting behaviours, I refrain from concluding that socioeconomic factors are the most dominant influences of them. To analyse the relationship further, I would have liked to look at outside influences like issue and tactical voting, bringing Brexit and the pandemic into the picture. But, these gatherings do reaffirm that election behaviour isn't always predictable, assumptions shouldn't necessarily be carried election to election, and parties shouldn't simply sit back and expect any 'safe seats'. If anything, recent elections have shown that anything is possible on election day.
